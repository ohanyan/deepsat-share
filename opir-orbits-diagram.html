OPIR Orbit Geometry: GEO vs HEO

Side view (Earth cross-section through the poles), roughly to scale
GEO satellites (36,000 km, over equator)
HEO / Molniya orbit (apogee over Arctic)
Polar coverage gap (invisible to GEO)
VLEO (DeepSat, for scale)

GEO (blue): Three satellites spaced around the equator at 36,000 km altitude can cover roughly all longitudes between ~70°N and ~70°S. They're geostationary — they stay fixed over one spot. But above 70° latitude, Earth's curvature blocks the line of sight. The poles are in the blind spot.

HEO / Molniya (orange): A stretched, tilted orbit. Apogee (high point) sits ~40,000 km above the Arctic. Because satellites move slow at apogee (Kepler's 2nd law), the satellite "hangs" over the pole for ~8 hours per 12-hour orbit. Two HEO satellites phased 180° apart provide continuous Arctic coverage 24/7.

Why this matters for missile warning: ICBMs from Russia take polar trajectories to reach CONUS. Without HEO coverage, there's no persistent IR stare during boost phase over the Arctic. This is exactly the coverage SBIRS HEO provides today (hosted on NRO satellites) and what Next-Gen OPIR Polar (NGP) is being built to replace.

Why polar ≠ VLEO: DeepSat operates in VLEO (250-350 km), far below either orbit shown here. Our mission is high-frequency ISR collection, not persistent IR stare. Different orbit, different sensor, different customer. When someone says "persistent" in a Space Force context, they likely mean OPIR.
